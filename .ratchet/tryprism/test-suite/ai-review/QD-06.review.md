## Evaluation Criteria

### 1. Motivation Targeting (weight: 35%)

The core difference between a good Enneagram question and a poor one: it should probe WHY (motivations, fears, desires), not just WHAT (surface behaviors).

**Good examples:**
- "I feel most at peace when I know I've done something right according to my own standards." (Type 1 — targets the motivation for inner integrity)
- "I often find myself wondering if people would still like me if I stopped helping them." (Type 2 — targets core fear of being unloved)
- "I tend to withdraw from others when I need to recharge my mental energy." (Type 5 — targets the withdrawal-to-preserve-energy pattern)

**Poor examples (behavioral-only):**
- "I work hard." (behavioral, doesn't distinguish types)
- "I like to be organized." (behavioral, many types could relate)
- "I help others when they need it." (social desirability — most people say yes)

For each type, evaluate whether the questions collectively distinguish THAT type from adjacent and contrasting types. A good Type 4 question should score higher for Type 4 than for Type 3 or Type 5.

### 2. Social Desirability Bias (weight: 30%)

A question has high social desirability bias if most people would answer it in the same direction regardless of their Enneagram type — because one answer is clearly "better."

**High-bias examples:**
- "I care about being honest." (Everyone wants to say yes — doesn't discriminate)
- "I try to be a good friend." (Universally endorsed)
- "I sometimes feel lonely." (Most people do; too universal)

**Low-bias examples:**
- "I would rather be right than popular." (Forces a choice between two values)
- "I often put off difficult emotions by keeping busy with new experiences." (The "yes" answer is neither clearly positive nor negative)
- "When I realize I've made a mistake, my first instinct is to immediately figure out how to correct it." (Natural for Type 1, less so for others)

Evaluate the proportion of questions that have a clearly "correct" or socially desirable answer. Target: fewer than 15% of questions should have obvious social desirability bias.

### 3. Cultural Sensitivity (weight: 20%)

Evaluate whether questions work for both Western (English) and Chinese (East Asian) cultural contexts:

**Western-centric red flags:**
- References to "raising your hand" in class or at work (different cultural norms)
- Assumptions about individualism vs. collectivism being universally shared
- Idioms that don't translate: "pulling yourself up by your bootstraps," "take the bull by the horns"
- References to therapy, counseling, or emotional processing as normal practices (varies by culture)
- Scenarios assuming nuclear family structure

**Good cross-cultural question design:**
- Uses universal human experiences (feeling misunderstood, desire for connection, fear of failure)
- Avoids specific cultural contexts (office meetings, dinner parties, dating apps)
- Focuses on inner states rather than culturally-specific situations

Evaluate: What percentage of questions are culture-agnostic vs. Western-centric?

### 4. Coverage and Facet Diversity (weight: 15%)

For each type, the 6 questions (quick mode) should cover different facets:

**Type 4 example facets:**
- Identity and uniqueness (not just being different, but needing to feel it)
- Emotional depth and intensity
- Aesthetic sensitivity
- Envy and comparison
- Fear of ordinariness
- Longing for what is absent

If all 6 Type 4 questions ask about "being different," that's insufficient facet coverage.

Evaluate: Do the questions for each type cover diverse facets, or do they feel repetitive?

## Specific Questions to Flag

During your review, flag any question that:
- Has a clear "right answer" (high social desirability)
- Could be answered the same way by 3+ Enneagram types (insufficient discriminating power)
- Is Western-centric or culturally inappropriate
- Probes only behaviors, not motivations
- Is confusing, double-barreled, or ambiguous in meaning
